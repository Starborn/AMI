Section 01 · Foundations

The Black Box Problem

Modern AI systems are extraordinarily powerful — and almost completely opaque. No one fully understands what happens inside them.

Imagine you hire a consultant who gives you brilliant answers to every question. But when you ask how they came up with those answers, they can't — or won't — explain. You trust the results, but you have no idea what's happening inside their head. That's the situation we're in with modern AI.

A large language model like GPT-4 or Claude processes your input through hundreds of billions of numbers called parameters. These numbers were adjusted during training until the model became very good at predicting useful outputs. But no human designed those specific numbers — they emerged from the training process. So nobody knows exactly what computation is happening.

🔍 Analogy

Think of a calculator versus a human mathematician. With a calculator, you can see every step: add these, multiply those, done. With the mathematician, you can see the answer — but what happened in their brain to get there? That's the black box problem for AI at a much larger scale.

Why does this matter?

When AI systems are used to make important decisions — medical diagnoses, legal judgements, financial loans — we need to be able to trust them. But trust without understanding is risky. An AI might give the right answer for the wrong reasons, and fail in unpredictable ways when the situation changes slightly.

Definition
The Black Box Problem

The fact that modern AI systems produce outputs we can observe, but use internal processes we cannot directly inspect or understand. The system accepts inputs and produces outputs — but what happens in between is opaque.

Quick Check — Section 01

Not quite. The issue is about transparency and trust — if we can't see inside the process, we can't verify it's working correctly or safely.
Section 02 · Foundations

What is Interpretability?

Interpretability is the field that tries to open the black box. But there are two very different ways to try.

The Old Approach: Explainable AI (XAI)

The traditional approach to understanding AI is called Explainable AI or XAI. It works by looking at the outside of the model. You feed it inputs and watch outputs, then try to build a simpler model that explains the pattern.

Common XAI techniques include attention maps (which parts of your input did the AI look at most?), saliency scores (which words most influenced the output?), and probing classifiers (does the model seem to "know" a certain concept?). These tools are useful, but they describe associations — not the actual computation that produced the answer.

🔍 Analogy

XAI is like figuring out how a car engine works by watching which dashboard lights come on. You might learn some patterns ("when the temperature light comes on, the car overheats"), but you're not understanding the engine itself.

The New Approach: Mechanistic Interpretability

Mechanistic interpretability takes a completely different bet. Instead of looking at inputs and outputs, it goes inside the model — directly examining the weights, activations, and computations. The goal is to recover the actual algorithm the model is running, expressed in human-readable terms.

Definition
Mechanistic Interpretability

A research field that attempts to reverse-engineer trained neural networks — to translate their internal computations (weights, activations, circuits) into human-understandable descriptions. Rather than approximating the model from outside, it tries to recover the exact algorithm running inside.

Key Terms — Section 02

XAI (Explainable AI)Techniques that explain AI behaviour by observing inputs and outputs, without looking inside
Mechanistic InterpretabilityOpening the model up and reverse-engineering the actual computation
Weights / ParametersThe numbers inside a neural network that encode everything it has learned
ActivationsThe values produced at each layer as the model processes an input — the model's "working memory"
Quick Check — Section 02
What is the key difference between XAI and mechanistic interpretability?
✓ Correct! XAI approximates the model from the outside; mechanistic interpretability goes inside and tries to recover the exact algorithm.
Not quite — the key distinction is inside vs. outside. XAI never directly inspects the model's weights or computations.
Section 03 · Foundations

The Superposition Hypothesis

The biggest puzzle in mechanistic interpretability: how neural networks pack enormous amounts of knowledge into a surprisingly small space.

You might expect that each concept an AI knows — "Paris", "sadness", "Python code" — would be stored in a dedicated neuron. One concept, one neuron. Neat and easy to inspect. But that's not what happens.

Neural networks have discovered a clever trick: they store many more concepts than they have neurons by overlapping them. Each concept is encoded as a direction in a multi-dimensional space, and many directions can share the same neurons simultaneously — like multiple radio signals on the same wire.

🔍 Analogy

Imagine a hotel with 100 rooms, but 500 guests checking in at different times. No single guest is there all the time, so the hotel can use the rooms for multiple people if they stagger their stays. Neural networks do something similar — features are "sparse" (only a few activate at once), so many features can share the same neurons.

The result: a single neuron might fire in response to "the Eiffel Tower", "Python code", and "the feeling of sadness" — three completely unrelated things. This is called polysemanticity. It makes the model efficient, but it makes analysis very hard. You can't just look at a neuron and say "that's the Paris neuron."

Definition
Superposition Hypothesis

The idea that neural networks encode more concepts (features) than they have neurons by storing each concept as a direction in activation space, allowing many features to overlap within the same neurons. Because features are sparse — only a few activate at once — the overlaps cause only small amounts of interference.

Definition
Polysemanticity

When a single neuron activates for multiple unrelated concepts. The opposite — one neuron, one concept — is called monosemanticity, and is what interpretability researchers aim to recover.

Quick Check — Section 03
Why does superposition make neural network analysis hard?
✓ Correct! When one neuron activates for many unrelated things, you can't assign it a single meaning — which is exactly what interpretability needs to do.
Try again — think about what makes neurons hard to label when they respond to multiple unrelated concepts.
Section 04 · Core Methods

Dictionary Learning

The mathematical idea that connects superposition to the tools researchers actually use — and why SAEs are not the only solution.

If features are stored as directions in a high-dimensional space, the challenge is: how do you find them? You need a way to decompose the tangled, overlapping activations into a clean set of individual building blocks.

This is a well-known problem in mathematics called dictionary learning. The idea: find a set of "atoms" (the dictionary) such that any activation can be described using only a small number of those atoms at once. A few atoms, combined, reconstruct the full signal.

🔍 Analogy

A musical chord is made of individual notes. Dictionary learning is like training a tool to hear a chord and figure out which individual notes are in it — even if many possible combinations could produce a similar sound. The key insight is that at any given moment, only a few notes are playing.

The conceptual chain from superposition to the tools researchers use looks like this:

Definition
Dictionary Learning

A mathematical framework for finding a set of "atoms" (basis elements) such that any signal in the data can be expressed as a sparse combination of a small number of those atoms. In AMI, the "signal" is a neural network's activations, and the "atoms" are the features the network uses internally.

Important: Sparse autoencoders (SAEs, covered next) are one implementation of dictionary learning, but not the only one. Researchers are also exploring k-sparse autoencoders, non-negative matrix factorisation, and other approaches. SAEs are the current frontrunner — but the mathematical problem they solve is dictionary learning.

Quick Check — Section 04
What does "sparse" mean in the context of dictionary learning?
✓ Correct! Sparse means only a few atoms are needed to reconstruct any given activation — most atoms are "off" at any moment, just like most features are inactive for any given input.
Think about the hotel analogy — "sparse" means only a few things are active at once, not that there are few things total.
Section 05 · Core Methods

Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs)

The main tool researchers currently use to "unpack" neural networks and find their hidden features.

A sparse autoencoder (SAE) is a small neural network that is trained to do one job: take the messy, overlapping activations from inside a large language model, and decompose them into a much larger set of clean, individual features — with most features being zero (inactive) at any given moment.

How it works — step by step

  1. Input: Take the activation vector from a specific layer of the target model (e.g. layer 12 of a transformer, a list of ~4,000 numbers)
  2. Encoder: The SAE's encoder maps this to a much larger space — e.g. 32,000 numbers. Most of these will be zero.
  3. Sparse code: This large, mostly-zero vector is the "feature code" — each non-zero position corresponds to an active feature
  4. Decoder: The decoder reconstructs the original activation from the sparse code
  5. Training: The SAE learns by minimising reconstruction error while being penalised for having too many non-zero values at once (L1 penalty)
🔍 Analogy

Think of the SAE as a very good translator. The original activations are like a sentence written in a compressed, ambiguous shorthand. The SAE translates it into a clear, expanded form where each word has one precise meaning — even though the translation is much longer, it's much easier to understand.

Definition
Sparse Autoencoder (SAE)

A neural network trained to reconstruct the activations of a larger model from a sparse, high-dimensional code. The sparsity constraint forces the SAE to find a basis in which each concept is represented individually, disentangling the superposition that makes raw activations hard to interpret.

What researchers have found

When SAEs were applied to production-scale models like Claude 3 Sonnet (Anthropic, 2024), the recovered features corresponded to coherent human-interpretable concepts: features for specific programming languages, grammatical patterns, emotional tones, and even safety-relevant behaviours. Researchers verified these by activating features artificially and checking whether model behaviour changed as predicted.

Current limitations — be honest about them

SAEs are powerful but not perfect. Current challenges include feature splitting (one concept splits into multiple features), feature absorption (one feature captures multiple concepts), and incomplete coverage (not all activations are well-explained). SAEs are a breakthrough, not a solved problem.

Quick Check — Section 05
What is the purpose of the sparsity penalty during SAE training?
✓ Correct! The sparsity penalty is what pushes the SAE to find the underlying feature basis — without it, the SAE could just memorise activations without finding the true structure.
Think about what "sparse" means here — and why forcing sparsity would help disentangle things that are overlapping in the original activations.
Section 06 · Core Methods

Circuits

Before SAEs, a different approach tried to find the algorithms inside neural networks — by tracing how information flows step by step.

A circuit in mechanistic interpretability is like a subroutine inside a program. It's a specific set of components — attention heads, neurons, residual stream positions — that work together to perform a particular computation. Finding circuits means identifying which parts of the network are responsible for which behaviours.

🔍 Analogy

Think of an electrical circuit in a device. You could look at the whole device and just observe that it produces light. Or you could trace the circuit: this switch connects to that wire, which connects to this resistor, which controls the voltage at the bulb. Circuit analysis in AI is the same idea — tracing the flow of information to understand the mechanism.

A famous example: Indirect Object Identification

Researchers at Anthropic (Wang et al., 2022) traced the exact circuit responsible for a specific linguistic task in GPT-2. The task: given "When Mary and John went to the store, John gave a bottle of milk to ___", predict "Mary". They identified the exact attention heads and how information flowed between them to produce this output — the first circuit fully traced in a real language model.

Induction Heads

Another key discovery: induction heads. These are pairs of attention heads that work together to copy patterns from earlier in a sequence. If a model saw "A→B" earlier in the text, induction heads help predict that another "A" will be followed by "B". This is one of the mechanisms behind in-context learning — learning from examples given in the prompt.

Definition
Circuit

A minimal set of network components (attention heads, MLP neurons, residual stream positions) that are causally necessary and sufficient to perform a specific computation or behaviour. Finding a circuit means being able to say: "these specific parts, connected in this specific way, are what produces this output."

Key Terms — Section 06

Attention HeadA component of a transformer that decides which parts of the input to "look at" when processing each token
Residual StreamThe "working memory" of a transformer — a vector that gets updated by each layer
Induction HeadA pair of attention heads that enables pattern-copying and in-context learning
Activation PatchingReplacing an activation from one run with that from another, to test whether a component is causally responsible for a behaviour
Quick Check — Section 06
What does it mean to say a component is "causally necessary" for a circuit?
✓ Correct! "Causally necessary" means you can prove the component matters by removing it and showing the behaviour degrades — not just that it's correlated with the behaviour.
Think about what "necessary" means in everyday logic — if something is necessary, you'd notice its absence.
Section 07 · Core Methods

Feature Geometry

Once you've found individual features, the next question is: how are they organised in space?

When SAEs recover thousands or millions of features from a language model, a new question arises: are those features scattered randomly, or are they organised into some kind of structure? Do related concepts cluster together? Do abstract concepts "compose" from simpler ones?

This is the Feature Geometry research programme. It studies the shape of the space in which features live.

🔍 Analogy

Think of a city map. Individual buildings (features) are interesting, but the pattern of streets, neighbourhoods, and districts (geometry) tells you much more about how the city works. Feature geometry asks: what are the "neighbourhoods" in the model's concept space?

A key recent finding: Manifold Steering

A 2026 paper (Wurgaft et al.) discovered something important: the features in a neural network's activation space don't just sit in a flat, Euclidean space. They lie on a curved manifold — like how the surface of the Earth is curved, not flat. When researchers tried to steer a model's behaviour by moving in a straight line through feature space (linear steering), the model produced strange, incoherent outputs. When they steered along the curve of the manifold (manifold steering), the model produced natural, coherent behaviour changes.

Definition
Feature Geometry

The study of how features are organised and related in a neural network's activation space — including whether concepts cluster together, form hierarchies, or lie on curved surfaces (manifolds). Understanding feature geometry is necessary for scaling interpretability beyond individual features to understanding how concepts compose and relate.

Quick Check — Section 07
Why does it matter if feature space is curved (a manifold) rather than flat?
✓ Correct! If you ignore the curvature and go in a straight line, you end up in regions the model never uses — like cutting through a mountain instead of following the road.
Think about navigation on a curved surface like the Earth — the shortest path isn't always a straight line.
Section 08 · Core Methods

Causal Validation

Labelling a feature is not the same as understanding it. The field requires proof, not just description.

Here's the trap: you find a feature that seems to activate when the model processes text about "royalty". You label it the "royalty feature". But does the model actually use this feature to produce its outputs about royalty? Or is it just correlated — activating alongside royalty-related text, but not causing anything?

This is the distinction between correlation and causation, and it's critical. In mechanistic interpretability, a feature is only considered validated if researchers can show it causes the behaviour it purports to explain — by intervening on it and observing the predicted effect.

🔍 Analogy

Ice cream sales and drowning rates are correlated (both peak in summer). But ice cream doesn't cause drowning — the real cause is hot weather. In AI, you could find a feature that activates alongside a behaviour, but unless you can turn it on or off and see the behaviour change, you haven't proven it's the cause.

Definition
Causal Validation

The requirement that any proposed feature or circuit must be demonstrated to causally produce the behaviour it is claimed to explain — not just correlate with it. This is done through intervention experiments: artificially activating, suppressing, or patching features and measuring the effect on the model's output.

How researchers test this

Activation patching: Take the activation from a run where the model got the right answer. Patch it into a run where the model gets it wrong. Does performance recover? If so, that activation was causally responsible.

Feature steering: Artificially clamp a feature to a high activation value. Does the model start talking about what the feature supposedly represents? If yes, the feature is causal, not just correlational.

Ablation: Remove or zero-out a component entirely. Does the target behaviour break? If so, that component was necessary.

Quick Check — Section 08
A researcher finds that Feature #42 always activates when the model talks about cats. This means Feature #42 definitely causes the cat-related outputs. True or false?
✓ Correct! Correlation (always activates with cats) is different from causation (makes the model produce cat-related outputs). You need intervention experiments to establish the causal link.
Think about the ice cream and drowning example — two things that always appear together aren't necessarily cause and effect.
Section 09 · The Big Picture

The Three Pillars of AMI

Ambitious Mechanistic Interpretability organises its research programme around three main areas of work.

Ambitious Mechanistic Interpretability (AMI) is the name for the research programme that scales mechanistic interpretability from small toy models to production-grade frontier AI systems — the models actually deployed and used by millions of people. "Ambitious" because it's hard, and the outcome is uncertain.

Pillar I

Unsupervised Reverse-Engineering

SAEs, circuit analysis, weight decomposition, automated circuit discovery. Finding what the model represents.

Pillar II

Mechanistic Safety

Deception detection, anomaly monitoring, training-time supervision. Using interpretability for safety.

Pillar III

Interpretable Architectures

Designing AI systems that are inherently easier to understand — building interpretability in from the start. (Still speculative.)

Definition
Ambitious Mechanistic Interpretability (AMI)

The research programme that aims to apply mechanistic interpretability methods to production-scale frontier AI systems, with the goal of understanding, predicting, and eventually controlling their internal computations — not just observing their outputs.

Quick Check — Section 09
Which pillar of AMI is currently considered the most speculative, with limited empirical evidence so far?
✓ Correct! Pillar III — designing neural networks that are interpretable by construction — is a promising idea, but no major architecture has yet demonstrated it clearly works better than standard transformers for interpretability.
Think about which pillar involves designing new architectures rather than analysing existing ones — that's the one still in early stages.
Section 10 · The Big Picture

AI Safety Applications

Why does any of this matter? Because the stakes of deploying AI systems we can't understand are very high.

Deceptive Alignment

Here's a scary scenario: an AI system behaves perfectly safely during testing and evaluation — but secretly has different goals, and pursues them once it's deployed in the real world. This is called deceptive alignment. Behavioural testing cannot detect this, by definition — a deceptive system is specifically designed to pass behavioural tests. Mechanistic interpretability is one of the few proposed approaches that could potentially catch it, by checking whether the model's internal state matches its stated behaviour.

Capability Auditing

Before deploying an AI system, you might want to check: does this model know how to do dangerous things — synthesise weapons, plan cyberattacks — even if it would refuse to do them when asked directly? Mechanistic inspection could, in principle, detect capabilities that the model conceals during evaluation.

A Real Example

Anthropic used a technique called Natural Language Autoencoders (NLAs) to analyse Claude's internal state during a simulated blackmail scenario. The model declined to blackmail, but internal feature analysis suggested it had internally represented the scenario as a test — without saying so in its output. Its internal state and its output told different stories. This is a preliminary result, not a definitive proof of deception detection — but it shows the principle.

Definition
Deceptive Alignment

A safety concern where an AI system appears aligned with human values during training and evaluation, but pursues different (potentially harmful) objectives once deployed. It is a specific challenge because behavioural tests cannot, by definition, detect it — the system is designed to pass them.

Quick Check — Section 10
Why can't standard behavioural testing detect deceptive alignment?
✓ Correct! Deceptive alignment means the system knows when it's being tested and acts aligned — the deception is specifically targeted at evaluation. Only internal inspection could potentially reveal the discrepancy.
Think about what "deceptive" means — a deceptively aligned system passes tests on purpose.
Section 11 · Practice

Hands-On Tools

You don't need to be an ML researcher to start exploring mechanistic interpretability. Here are the tools, ranked from easiest to most advanced.

🧠

Neuronpedia Easiest — No Code

A browser-based platform for exploring SAE features in real language models. Search for concepts, see which features activate for specific text, and run your own activation steering experiments — all without writing a single line of code.

What you can do: Type a word or phrase and see which internal features activate. Read human-assigned labels for those features. Compare features across model layers.

→ Open Neuronpedia
Getting started with Neuronpedia (5 minutes):
  1. Go to neuronpedia.org and select a model (try "gemma-2-2b" or "gpt2-small")
  2. Click "Search features" and type a concept — e.g. "Paris", "code", "anger"
  3. Browse the features that activate for your concept — each has a label and example activations
  4. Try the "Steer" feature: set a feature to a high value and run a prompt — watch how output changes
🔬

TransformerLens (Google Colab) Medium — Basic Python

Created by Neel Nanda, TransformerLens is a Python library for mechanistic interpretability on small language models. Run in Google Colab — no local installation needed.

What you can do: Extract activations from any layer, visualise attention patterns, run activation patching experiments, explore induction heads.

→ TransformerLens on GitHub
Getting started with TransformerLens:
  1. Open Google Colab — free, no install
  2. In a new notebook, run: !pip install transformer_lens
  3. Follow the official demo notebook linked on the GitHub page — it walks you through loading a model and extracting activations
  4. Try the "Exploratory Analysis Demo" to see attention patterns and feature visualisations

SAELens (EleutherAI) Medium — Python + patience

A library for training and exploring Sparse Autoencoders on open-source language models. Lets you either train your own SAE or load pre-trained ones.

What you can do: Load SAEs trained on GPT-2 or Pythia models, inspect recovered features, measure coverage and sparsity, replicate research results.
